About Me

Introduction

Hello! I am a current undergraduate student at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute (Troy, NY) studying for my Bachelors in Computer Science major. For now, I am studying on the General Track, although after completing Intro to Algorithms, I would like to concentrate on the Software Engineering and Artificial Intelligence track. I am also a front-end software developer for the RPI HEALS Project, which seeks to improve the healthcare industry by helping patients manage their health conditions, help doctors find abnormalities through the use of statistics, cloud computing, and data representation, and decrease the costs of healthcare. I am a passionate software developer who has been programming since the sixth grade, and am determined to write clean, reliable software.

Middle School

My passion for computer science began in middle school, when I joined the Technology Student Association. I was the new kid in school, and joined the STEM-oriented club to make new friends and explore different hobbies. As an active member of the Technology Student Association, I took part in many STEM-related activities, such as Dragster Design, Website Design, and Video Game Design. I had showed a natural skill in the area of software development as well, winning numerous competitions at TSA's Annual Connecticut State Conference, where our projects are judged based on creativity, functionality, and documentation.

My passion for computer science grew as I had the wonderful opportunity to attend TSA's 2015 National Conference in Dallas, Texas. There, I was amazed by the vast amount of technical projects that students from all over the country submitted for display. It varied from robotics, software development, hackathons, too many to count! I was truly inspired to be surrounded by thousands of other young students who all had an enjoyment of STEM. I returned from the conference with a newfound appreciation for STEM, and convinced that I wanted to study the field of Computer Science.

High School

I entered high school excited to learn more about computer science! I was fortunate enough to learn at a high school with a great amount of computer science courses. Determined to learn as much as I could, I enrolled in every computer science course that was taught, including Intro to Computer Science, Exploring Computer Science, AP Computer Science Principles, Game and Web Design, and AP Computer Science A; and aced them.

I remained an active member of TSA, continuing my membership to the high school level. I took part in many different competitions and designed many different applications. I encouraged my friends to join the club, and volunteered to help with preparing our school for the Connecticut State Conference. During my senior year (2018-2019), I was elected to be the Connecticut TSA State Treasurer, where I helped organize and manage TSA at the state-level. As a State-Wide role model and leader, I represented Connecticut TSA at the state-level capital, regularly met with the other Connecticut TSA State Officers, managed the funds of Connecticut TSA, and prepare for the Annual Connecticut State Conference. As the Treasurer, I prioritized increasing awareness of Connecticut TSA (particularly in low-income and minority areas) through merchendice, as well as established our partnership with the American Cancer Society through a bake sale (with the help of volunteers from my high school's National Honor Society).

During my senior year of high school, I also helped one of my computer science teachers, Ms. Corricelli, to develop a new computer science course that specializes in cryptography and cybersecurity. During the spring semester, I volunteered during my lunch periods to review the course material with my teacher, as well as made adjustments to the course when needed. The course, Cryptography and Cybersecurity, specializes in the history of cryptography and cybersecurity, learning different ciphers, and learning how to program (in Python) the computer to encrypt and decrypt messages. This course is currently being offered at Conard High School.

I have also volunteered for various STEM organizations during the 2018-2019 academic year. In the spring semester, I volunteered to teach Scratch (block-based programming platform) to elementary school students at Smith Elementary School, as a member of Bits and Bytes, a Computer Science community service club. Additionally, I volunteered to join the National Girl's Collaborative Project's National Advisory Board, which advocates for female and non-binary students pursuing a STEM career.

I graduated from Conard High School with a 4.1/4.5 GPA and a member of the Class of 2019. I was accepted and enrolled into Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute for a Bachelors in Computer Science, and with AP credit, was able to start a semester ahead.

College

I started college a semester ahead, and did not waste any time to learn computer science. With Computer Science 1 completed in high school, I continued my studies through Data Structures, Calculus I & II, Foundations of Computer Science, and Computer Organization throughout my freshman year. Despite the average Fall 2019 Freshman Cumulative GPA of ~2.3, I finished my first semester with a 3.5 Cumulative GPA and made the Dean's Honor List.

As a freshman, I joined RPI's Coding && Community club, a STEM-oriented community service club that seeks to aid underrepresented students in STEM be exposed to and follow their passions in STEM. During the 2019-2020 academic school year, I volunteered as a Java Instructor at Troy High School, where I helped students create and debug their code. Additionally, in the summer of 2020, I volunteered as an Instructor for the STEP Summer 2020 program, which students can learn various aspects of computer science. As an instructor, I created some of the curriculum that was taught in the STEP Summer 2020 program, including Cryptography and Cybersecurity.

Another club that I joined in my freshman year was the American Computing Machinery - Women's Division (ACM-W) at RPI. ACM-W at RPI helps incoming female and non-binary students transition from high school to college, as well as establishes a support group for underrepresented students in Computer Science.

The Future

After completing my computer science degree, I would like to enter the workforce as a Full-Stack Software Engineer, and develop a specialization as I gain new work experience. At the moment, I am still exploring different areas of software development, and I am open to trying new things!

Additionally, I would like to expand my academic knowledge of computer science and further contribute to software-related research through a Masters in Computer Science.
